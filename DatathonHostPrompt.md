Data Never Dies
DND is the Spring 2026 Datathon hosted by CS and BA clubs. This datathon is hosted to provide an opportunity to students to dive deeper into a dataset and their analysis.


Prompt
Beyond: Distance, Access, and Outcome
Over the world, health outcomes do not appear evenly distributed. Some regions consistently report better long-term outcomes, lower preventable hospitalization rates, and higher continuity of care, while others show patterns of strain that are more difficult to explain.
Public discussions often point to geography, rural versus urban, proximity to hospitals, infrastructure quality, but the relationships are not straightforward. In some areas, facilities are nearby yet outcomes lag. In others, communities far from major centers perform better than expected.
Available datasets include utilization patterns, geographic indicators, and outcome metrics. However, reporting practices vary, and regional averages may obscure important differences within communities.
As policymakers and healthcare leaders debate whether resources should be reallocated, expanded, or restructured, questions remain about what is truly driving disparities, and what the data can reasonably support.

Dissect the Prompt
Figure out the different definitions of key words within the prompts and their titles.
Try to pick out any assumptions, generalizations, or uncertainties within the prompt.
Find out if there are correlations and data that are suitable for that prompt
Ensure that each framing question is specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-bound


Analytical Questions for the Prompt

Why?
Can help teams find their framing questions and answer more technical and analytical analysis questions we wish to see within their presentations.

Questions
Are outcome disparities consistent across geographic classifications, or do patterns shift when examined more closely?
How does distance to care relate to utilization or outcomes, and does that relationship vary by region or population?
Are there communities that defy common assumptions about access?
How sensitive are conclusions to how geographic categories are defined (e.g., distance bands, urban/rural cutoffs)?
Where does uncertainty increase due to sparse reporting or small population sizes?
What conclusions would be premature given the available data?
